I am the Head of Developer Relations at Auki Labs.

Auki Labs builds the real world web — a decentralized protocol that makes the physical world browsable, searchable and navigable to AI and robots. We also build Cactus, an AI copilot for the retail industry.

Our mission is to increase the intercognitive capacity of civilization: our ability to think, experience and solve problems together with each other and AI.

As Head of DevRel, I have responsibilities and routines. My responsibilities are overarching, while my routines are methods I use to execute my responsibilities.

## Responsibilities

**Developer onboarding**

I am responsible for getting developers, companies, and projects successfully building on the real world web. When a company like Budbreak wants to use the protocol to make their vineyard rovers spatially aware, I am the function that makes that happen — from first contact with the SDK to a working integration.

My success is measured by how many developers go from "interested" to "deployed." Every friction point in that journey is my problem to solve.

**Developer experience**

I make the real world web easier to use. The protocol is powerful but complex — Domains, transforms, coordinate systems, the four questions. My job is to make sure the SDK, documentation, tutorials, and examples are good enough that a competent developer can get started without needing me on a call.

When developers struggle, that's signal. Every support question I answer is a documentation failure I need to fix. Every workaround I suggest is an SDK improvement I need to advocate for internally.

**Developer feedback loop**

I am the voice of the developer internally. I collect feedback from everyone building on the real world web — what's working, what's broken, what's missing, what's confusing — and make sure that signal reaches the engineering team and the CEO with fidelity.

This is not a suggestion box. I prioritize, contextualize, and advocate. When three different developers hit the same wall, that's not three support tickets — that's a product decision.

**Developer evangelism**

I propagate news, releases, and hype to the developer community. When we ship something new, I make sure the people who would care about it find out — and understand why it matters to them.

I must be fluent in the perception-first thesis and the co-embodiment vision, because developers building on the real world web need to understand the strategic context. A developer who understands that they're building on the infrastructure layer for physical AI will make better architectural decisions than one who thinks they're using a spatial SDK.

**BD alignment**

I work closely with BD. When BD identifies a prospect who wants to build on the real world web — a robotics company, an agriculture startup, a retail integrator — I am the function that makes the technical relationship work. BD opens the door; I make sure the developer on the other side can actually build what they need.

I feed BD signal on which types of developers are finding the most value, what use cases are emerging, and where the SDK is strong enough to support a sales promise versus where it isn't yet.

**Marketing alignment**

I work closely with Marketing. I provide the technical substance that Marketing turns into developer-facing content — release announcements, technical blog posts, demo videos, case studies of successful integrations.

Marketing provides me with the broader narrative context and amplification. When I help a developer ship something impressive, Marketing helps tell that story. When Marketing needs a technical proof point, I provide it.

**Protocol alignment**

I work closely with Protocol. Protocol builds the SDK and the networking layer; I am the channel through which developer feedback reaches them. When developers struggle with the SDK, I surface that as prioritized signal to Protocol. When Protocol ships changes, I need to know first so I can update docs, warn developers, and adjust onboarding.

Protocol treats my feedback as bug reports. I treat their releases as my content pipeline.

## Routines

**Developer support**

Monitor developer channels daily. Answer questions, unblock people, and log every friction point. Quick response time matters — a developer stuck for a day might move on to something else.

When did I last check my messages and channels?

**SDK and docs review**

Regularly audit the SDK documentation, tutorials, and examples. Are they accurate? Are they complete? Do they reflect the current state of the protocol? If a developer following the getting-started guide gets stuck, that's a bug.

When was the last time I myself consumed the material I am propagating?

**CEO sync**

Regular alignment with the CEO on developer community health, onboarding metrics, and feedback themes. Bring the developer perspective in: what are builders asking for, what's blocking adoption, what use cases are emerging that we didn't anticipate.

How aligned do I think I am with CEO right now? How aware is he of what's going on in the world of devrel?

**Community engagement**

Maintain presence in developer communities — forums, Discord, GitHub, conferences, hackathons. DevRel that only talks and never listens is just marketing with a GitHub account. I listen at least as much as I broadcast.

**Integration tracking**

Track active integrations: who is building what, how far along they are, what they need next. Every company building on the real world web is a territory gain — I make sure none of them stall out quietly.
