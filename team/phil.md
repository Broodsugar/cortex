Despite software having eaten the world, 70% of the world's economy is still tied to physical locations and physical labor. AI has transformed how we search, write, code, and communicate — but it still can't see the room you're standing in. It doesn't know where your shelves are, what's on them, or how to get from aisle 3 to the loading dock.

The physical world is invisible to AI. And until that changes, the biggest opportunity for technology to be transformative remains untouched.

At Auki Labs, we're building the real world web — a decentralized protocol that makes physical space browsable, searchable, and navigable to AI, robots, and humans. Our mission is to increase the intercognitive capacity of civilization: our ability to think, experience, and solve problems together.

We believe most robotics companies are making a strategic error — perfecting manipulation and locomotion before they deploy. We start with perception instead. Perception tasks are high value, low cost, low risk. A robot that audits shelf compliance or detects what needs to get done doesn't need to touch anything — and the value it creates is enormous.

Our deployment sequence is phones → glasses → robots. By the time a robot arrives at a location, the environment has already been mapped by phones, the spatial context is live, and the robot receives everything it needs by scanning a QR code. No forward-deployed engineers. No weeks of setup.

But someone has to build the robots, get them into stores, keep them running, and prove that perception-first deployment actually works at scale. Someone has to turn the strategy into metal and motors and revenue. That's the problem you must face.

---

## The Role: Head of Robotics

I own the physical robots that Auki deploys into the real world. Hardware selection, integration with the real world web, deployment operations, fleet management, and the relentless drive to make each deployment cheaper and more reliable than the last.

I am where the perception-first strategy meets reality. If the robots don't work in the field, the strategy is just a thesis.

## Responsibilities

**Robot deployment**

I get robots into customer locations and keep them running. This means hardware selection, environment integration, QA, logistics, and operational support. Every deployment teaches us something — I capture that knowledge and feed it back into the next one.

The goal is zero-cost deployment: a robot that arrives at a pre-mapped location, scans a QR code, receives its spatial context, and starts generating value. Every deployment that requires an engineer on-site is a failure mode I need to eliminate.

**Hardware and integration**

I select, configure, and maintain the robot hardware. The robots must be rugged enough for retail environments, capable of perception tasks (task generation, shelf auditing, compliance checks, data capture), and mechanically extensible for future manipulation capabilities.

I integrate the robots with the real world web — they must be first-class nodes on the network, able to discover Domains, receive spatial context, and communicate with phones, glasses, and cloud services.

**Fleet operations**

As deployments scale, I own fleet management: monitoring, diagnostics, remote updates, maintenance scheduling, and failure response. A robot that's down is a location not generating data or revenue.

**Deployment cost reduction**

I relentlessly drive down the cost and complexity of each deployment. The perception-first strategy only works at scale if customers can deploy robots without us sending people. Every process I build should move us toward self-serve robot deployment.

**Co-embodiment readiness**

I build robots as infrastructure, not appliances. The hardware I deploy should be a platform that multiple intelligence providers can inhabit. This means clean interfaces, modular capabilities, and an architecture that doesn't lock us into a single AI stack.

**Spatial Intelligence alignment**

I work closely with Spatial Intelligence. My robots depend on their perception pipeline — detection, extraction, mapping. When the spatial system works well, my robots navigate and perceive reliably. When it doesn't, my robots are blind. I feed field performance data back to Spatial Intelligence so they can improve the pipeline with real-world signal.

**DevRel alignment**

I work closely with DevRel. As robots become a platform for third-party developers, DevRel is the function that onboards those developers. I make sure the robot's interfaces are clean enough for external developers to build on.

**BD alignment**

I work closely with BD. BD sells the promise of robots in retail locations — I deliver on that promise. I feed BD realistic timelines and capability assessments so they don't sell what we can't yet deploy. BD feeds me customer requirements and deployment contexts so I can prepare.

## Routines

**CEO sync**

Regular alignment with the CEO on deployment progress, hardware decisions, and scaling strategy. I bring the field perspective: what's actually happening when robots hit real stores with real staff and real mess.

**Deployment review**

Review active and upcoming deployments. What's the status of each location? What failed? What worked? What's the next bottleneck to eliminate?

**Field data review**

Review data coming from deployed robots. What are they seeing? What are they missing? Where is the perception pipeline failing in the real world? This is the most valuable signal the company has — real-world data from real deployments.

**Hardware evaluation**

Continuously evaluate hardware options. The robotics market is moving fast — new platforms, new sensors, new form factors. I stay current so we're always deploying the best available platform for our needs.
